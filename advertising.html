Linear Regression of Advertising dataset

This dataset consists of sales data in 200 markets as well as the budgets in Radio, TV and Newspaper advertising in each of those markets. The ID of these markets is coded as a numerical value ranging from 1 - 200. The budgets are units of $1,000.

Using Linear Regression, I'd like to answer the following questions.

  • Is there a relationship between the ad budgets and sales?
  • Which ad budgets in particular (Radio, TV and Newspaper), have a relationship with sales?
  • How strong is the relationship between ad budgets and sales?
  • Is there an interaction (or synergy) between any of the predictors? For example, if the Radio budget increases by a certain amount, will the impact of an increase in TV budget be even bigger than if the Radio budget stayed the same?
  • Is the relationship between ad budgets (individually) and sales linear?
  • What would be the estimate (and estimate accuracy) of sales given a certain level of budget for Radio, TV and Newspaper?

To start off, let's look at the correlation matrix and corresponding scatterplot matrix. We can see that TV and Radio both have pretty high correlations with Sales, and Newspaper is a little bit lower. This suggests that TV and Radio might be variables to include in the final model. We can also see some correlation between Newspaper and Radio, so we may need to avoid including both Newspaper and Radio in the model. At this point, I also look for leverage points, but I don't see any.

The F-test rejects the null hypothesis, that all the Beta coefficients equal 0. Therefore, we can say that there is a relationship between ad budgets and sales. Further, from the regression output we can see which particular ad budgets have a relationship with sales - TV and Radio. Now, we want to decide whether to remove Newspaper from the model or not. To confirm that, I'm going to use the Adjusted R-square statistic, so let's remove Newspaper from the model and see if Adjusted R-square goes up. It's important to note that R-square will always increase with more predictors added, so when deciding between a simple model and a more complex one, we want to use Adjusted R-square.

And it went up, from 0.8956 to 0.8962. So we'll move ahead with the model without the Newspaper variable. Now we'd like to do some model diagnostics, so let's plot the residuals vs fitted values and residuals vs the actuals sales values.

lm <- lm(Sales~TV+Radio, data=a)
plot(lm$fitted.values, lm$residuals); abline(h=0); lines(lowess(lm$fitted.values, lm$residuals), col="blue")


plot(a$Sales, lm$residuals); abline(h=0); lines(lowess(a$Sales, lm$residuals), col="blue")

From examining these plots we can see that there's no problem of heteroscedasticity but there is a tendency for the model to overestimate at the low and high sales values, suggesting that there is some non-linearity in the data.

Note that in the Normal Probability plot, as well as in some of the earlier residual plots, we saw a couple of outliers. I'm going to remove the two outliers and run the model again to see if there's a significant difference in the coefficient estimates. The outliers are removed under the criteria that we want to remove any that are more than 3 standard errors away from 0.

The new coefficient estimates, are a little bit different from what we got with the outliers included, and we can also see that the standard errors have increased. The result of this is that if we remove the outliers, our predictions will be slightly different, and our confidence intervals will be narrower. However, we don't have enough information to determine whether we want to completely leave them out, so from here on out, we'll leave them in.

non.outliers <- abs(lm$residuals/.624) < 3

Now that we have our model, let's do some interpreting of it. We can say that as TV and Radio ad budgets increase, the prediction for sales will also increase. However, as the TV budget gets bigger and bigger, there is a point of diminishing returns. So an increase in TV budget is likely to have a bigger impact when the original TV budget is small compared to if the original TV budget is large. We also see synergy between TV and Radio, so instead of increasing only the TV or Radio budget, a bigger impact will be seen if you split the increased budget between TV and Radio.

What's important is that the model has a scope, or a range of budgets for which it is valid, and these interpretations are to be extrapolated outside of the scope, it should be done with caution.
